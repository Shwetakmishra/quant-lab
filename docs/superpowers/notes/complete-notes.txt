1   NUMBER PROPERTIES & SETS
Number types
Q.  What are natural (counting) numbers?
 
A.  1, 2, 3, 4, … (start at 1, no zero).
 
Q.  What are whole numbers?
 
A.  0, 1, 2, 3, … (counting numbers + 0).
 
Q.  What are integers?
 
A.  …, −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, … (whole numbers + negatives; extend both directions).
 
Q.  Definition of a rational number?
 
A.  Any number that can be written as a fraction a/b (b≠0). Includes all integers and terminating/repeating
 decimals.
Q.  Definition of an irrational number?
 
A.  A number that cannot be written as a fraction. Its decimal is non-terminating AND non-repeating (e.g. π,
 √2, e).
Q.  Is 0.333… rational or irrational?
 
A.  Rational (= 1/3). ‘Goes forever’ alone doesn’t make it irrational — it must also be non-repeating.
 
Q.  What is the full number hierarchy?
 
A.  Complex ⊇ Real ⊇ Rational ⊇ Integers. Real = anything on the number line; Complex adds imaginary (i =
 √−1).
Fractions
Q.  Proper fraction?
 
A.  Numerator < Denominator; value < 1.
 
Q.  Improper fraction?
 
A.  Numerator ≥ Denominator; value ≥ 1 (e.g. 5/5 = 1 still counts as improper).
 
Q.  Mixed number?
 
A.  A whole number + a proper fraction (e.g. 2 1/3); value > 1.
 
Q.  When does a fraction give a terminating decimal?
 
A.  When the denominator (in lowest terms) factors into only 2s and 5s. Any other prime factor → repeating.
 (Reduce first.)
Q.  How to convert a fully-repeating decimal (e.g. 0.ababab…) to a fraction?
 
A.  Numerator = the repeating block (ab); denominator = a 9 for each repeating digit. So 0.ababab… = ab/99.
 
Q.  How to convert a mixed repeating decimal (e.g. 0.2141414… where 2 is fixed, 14 repeats) to a fraction?
 
A.  Numerator = (all decimal digits, one cycle) − (the non-repeating digits) = 214 − 2 = 212. Denominator = a 9
 per repeating digit + a 0 per non-repeating digit = 990. So = 212/990.
Properties of 0 & 1
Q.  What is 00?
 
A.  Undefined (the x0=1 rule needs a nonzero base).
 
Q.  What is 0!?
 
A.  1 (by definition).
 
Q.  Key facts about 0?
 
A.  Multiple of every integer; factor of itself only; neither positive nor negative; even.

2   FACTORS, MULTIPLES & DIVISIBILITY
Q.  Number of factors of pa·qb?
 
A.  (a+1)(b+1). Extend by multiplying (exponent+1) for each prime.
 
Q.  How to count ODD factors of a number?
 
A.  Strip out all the 2s from the prime factorization, then count the factors of what remains.
 
Q.  How to count EVEN factors?
 
A.  Total factors − odd factors.
 
Q.  Why only strip 2s for odd factors?
 
A.  2 is the only even prime; odd factors contain no 2s.
 
Q.  Divisibility by 2?
 
A.  Last digit is even.
 
Q.  Divisibility by 3?
 
A.  Digit sum is divisible by 3.
 
Q.  Divisibility by 4?
 
A.  Last 2 digits form a number divisible by 4.
 
Q.  Divisibility by 5?
 
A.  Last digit is 0 or 5.
 
Q.  Divisibility by 6?
 
A.  Divisible by both 2 AND 3.
 
Q.  Divisibility by 8?
 
A.  Last 3 digits divisible by 8.
 
Q.  Divisibility by 9?
 
A.  Digit sum is divisible by 9.
 
Q.  Divisibility by 11?
 
A.  Alternating sum of digits (+−+−) is a multiple of 11 (including 0).
 
Q.  Remainder when dividing by 3 or 9?
 
A.  Same remainder as the digit sum gives (digit sum mod 3, or mod 9).
 
Q.  Remainder when dividing by 4 / 8 / 10?
 
A.  Use last 2 digits (÷4), last 3 digits (÷8), or the unit digit (÷10).
 
Q.  Remainder of x÷y when y > x?
 
A.  The remainder is x itself; quotient is 0.
 
Q.  HCF via ladder method?
 
A.  Divide only while a number divides EVERY term; stop when no common divisor; multiply all left-side
 divisors.
Q.  LCM via ladder method?
 
A.  Divide while it divides AT LEAST ONE term; continue until the bottom row is all 1s; multiply all left-side
 divisors.
Q.  What does it mean for two numbers to be co-prime?
 
A.  They share no common factor except 1, i.e. their HCF = 1 (e.g. 8 & 15).
 
Q.  How to test if n is prime?
 
A.  Test divisibility only by primes ≤ √n. If none divide it, n is prime.
 
Q.  Number of trailing zeros in n!?
 
A.  Count factors of 5: floor(n/5) + floor(n/25) + floor(n/125) + … (Legendre’s formula; floor each).
 
Q.  Why count 5s (not 2s) for trailing zeros?
 
A.  A trailing zero needs a 10 = 2×5. 2s are plentiful; 5s are the bottleneck.
 
Q.  What does the floor function (the [ ] brackets) do?
 
A.  Rounds DOWN to the nearest integer. For negatives: floor(−2.3) = −3.

3   ARITHMETIC & ALGEBRA
Exponents & identities
Q.  xa · xb = ?
 
A.  xa+b (same base, multiply → add exponents).
 
Q.  xa / xb = ?
 
A.  xa−b (same base, divide → subtract exponents).
 
Q.  xa · ya = ?
 
A.  (xy)a (same exponent → multiply bases).
 
Q.  x−n = ?
 
A.  1/xn (negative exponent → reciprocal).
 
Q.  (a+b)3 = ?
 
A.  a3 + 3a2b + 3ab2 + b3 (all plus).
 
Q.  (a−b)3 = ?
 
A.  a3 − 3a2b + 3ab2 − b3 (alternating signs).
 
Q.  (a−b)2 = ?
 
A.  a2 − 2ab + b2.
 
Q.  (a+b)(a−b) = ?
 
A.  a2 − b2 (difference of squares).
 
Q.  √(a2) = ?
 
A.  |a| (absolute value — not a, not ±a).
 
Q.  What makes an expression a polynomial?
 
A.  Every variable’s exponent is a whole number ≥ 0 (no 1/x, no √x). Constants and roots on coefficients are
 fine.
Quadratics
Q.  Quadratic formula?
 
A.  x = [−b ± √(b2−4ac)] / 2a.
 
Q.  What is the discriminant and what does it tell you?
 
A.  b2−4ac. >0 → 2 real roots; =0 → 1; <0 → 0 real roots.
 
Q.  Sum of roots of ax2+bx+c?
 
A.  −b/a.
 
Q.  Product of roots?
 
A.  c/a.
 
Q.  Vertex x-coordinate / axis of symmetry?
 
A.  x = −b/2a (also = average of the two roots).
 
Q.  Completing the square — method & use?
 
A.  Take half the x-coefficient → (x+h)2+k. Vertex (−h, k); min value = k (since a square is ≥ 0).
 
Q.  √36 vs solving x2 = 36?
 
A.  The √ symbol gives +6 only (positive root). Solving x2=36 gives ±6 (both).
 
Inequalities, ratios, interest
Q.  When do you flip an inequality sign?
 
A.  Only when multiplying/dividing both sides by a NEGATIVE number.
 
Q.  Order of a, a2, √a for a fraction (0<a<1)?
 
A.  √a > a > a2 (squaring → toward 0; rooting → toward 1).
 
Q.  Order of a, a2, √a for a>1?
 
A.  a2 > a > √a.
 
Q.  Percent increase / decrease formula?
 
A.  |difference| / starting value × 100. (Increase → smaller number in denominator; decrease → larger.)

Q.  Quick way to apply a % change?
 
A.  +x% → ×(1 + x/100); −x% → ×(1 − x/100).
 
Q.  Simple interest formula?
 
A.  SI = prt (gives interest; add P for the total amount).
 
Q.  Compound interest formula?
 
A.  A = P(1 + r/n)nt. n = times compounded per year; A = final amount; subtract P for interest.
 
Q.  Mixture / concentration?
 
A.  (component ÷ total) × 100 = % of that component.
 
4   SEQUENCES & SERIES
Q.  Arithmetic sequence?
 
A.  Add/subtract a constant (common difference) each term.
 
Q.  Geometric sequence?
 
A.  Multiply/divide by a constant (common ratio) each term.
 
Q.  Harmonic sequence?
 
A.  Reciprocals of an arithmetic sequence (e.g. 1, 1/2, 1/3, 1/4, …).
 
Q.  Quadratic sequence?
 
A.  Second differences are constant (first differences are not); formula involves n2.
 
Q.  Sequence vs series?
 
A.  Sequence = a list (2,4,6). Series = the sum of that list (2+4+6 = 12).
 
Q.  Number of terms in a spaced/evenly-spaced series?
 
A.  (Last − First)/step + 1. Snap endpoints inward to real multiples first if needed.
 
Q.  Sum of an arithmetic series?
 
A.  Sn = (n/2)(a + l): n = number of terms, a = first, l = last.
 
Q.  Sum of a geometric series?
 
A.  Sn = a(rn−1)/(r−1). n = number of terms (count them).
 
Q.  Sum of an infinite geometric series (|r|<1)?
 
A.  S = a/(1−r) (terms shrink to 0, so the sum converges).
 
5   COORDINATE GEOMETRY
Q.  Slope formula?
 
A.  (y2−y1)/(x2−x1) = rise/run.
 
Q.  Slope of a horizontal line (y = constant)?
 
A.  0.
 
Q.  Slope of a vertical line (x = constant)?
 
A.  Undefined (run = 0).
 
Q.  Slope-intercept form?
 
A.  y = mx + c (m = slope, c = y-intercept).
 
Q.  Point-slope form — and when to use it?
 
A.  y − y1 = m(x − x1). Use when you know the slope and one point (no +c needed).
 
Q.  Slopes of parallel lines?
 
A.  Equal slopes, different y-intercepts.
 
Q.  Slopes of perpendicular lines?
 
A.  Negative reciprocals (their product = −1).
 
Q.  Slope of a parabola ax2+bx+c at a point?
 
A.  2ax + b (plug in x). At the vertex (x=−b/2a), slope = 0.
 
Q.  Distance between two points?
 
A.  √[(x2−x1)2 + (y2−y1)2] (subtract first, then square).

Q.  Midpoint between two points?
 
A.  ((x1+x2)/2, (y1+y2)/2) — the average of the x’s and the average of the y’s.
 
Q.  Equation of a circle?
 
A.  (x−h)2 + (y−k)2 = r2; center (h,k), radius r.
 
Q.  Reflection across y = x?
 
A.  (x, y) → (y, x).
 
Q.  Reflection across y = −x?
 
A.  (x, y) → (−y, −x).
 
Q.  Reflection across horizontal line y = k?
 
A.  (x, y) → (x, 2k−y).
 
Q.  Reflection across vertical line x = k?
 
A.  (x, y) → (2k−x, y).
 
Q.  90° rotation anticlockwise about origin?
 
A.  (x, y) → (−y, x).
 
Q.  90° rotation clockwise about origin?
 
A.  (x, y) → (y, −x).
 
Q.  180° rotation about origin?
 
A.  (x, y) → (−x, −y).
 
Q.  Which way does a parabola open?
 
A.  a > 0 → up; a < 0 → down.
 
Q.  Find the y-intercept / x-intercept of a graph?
 
A.  y-intercept: set x = 0. x-intercept: set y = 0.
 
Q.  Graph shift: y = (x−h)2 + k from y = x2?
 
A.  Inside (horizontal) is backwards: −h → right h. Outside (vertical): +k → up k. Vertex (h, k).
 
6   GEOMETRY — TRIANGLES, POLYGONS & SOLIDS
Polygons
Q.  Sum of interior angles of an n-sided polygon?
 
A.  (n−2) × 180°. (It splits into n−2 triangles.)
 
Q.  Each interior angle of a regular polygon?
 
A.  (n−2) × 180 / n.
 
Q.  Sum of exterior angles of any polygon?
 
A.  360° (always, regardless of n).
 
Q.  Each exterior angle of a regular polygon?
 
A.  360 / n.
 
Triangles
Q.  The four Pythagorean triples?
 
A.  3-4-5, 5-12-13, 8-15-17, 7-24-25 (largest = hypotenuse) + all their multiples.
 
Q.  Side ratio of a 45-45-90 triangle?
 
A.  x : x : x√2 (hypotenuse = leg × √2).
 
Q.  Side ratio of a 30-60-90 triangle?
 
A.  x : x√3 : 2x (opposite the 30° : 60° : 90° angles).
 
Q.  What makes two triangles similar?
 
A.  Same shape, different size: equal angles, proportional sides.
 
Q.  Area ratio of two similar triangles?
 
A.  (side ratio)2 (area is 2D, so it scales by the square).
 
Q.  Criteria to prove similarity?
 
A.  AA, SAS, SSS (sides proportional). AA alone is enough.

Q.  Criteria to prove congruence?
 
A.  SSS, SAS, ASA, AAS (sides equal). AA is NOT enough.
 
Q.  Area of an equilateral triangle (side x)?
 
A.  (√3/4) × x2.
 
Circles
Q.  Central angle vs inscribed angle?
 
A.  Central angle = 2 × inscribed angle (on the same arc). Central vertex at center; inscribed vertex on the
 circle.
Q.  Sector area / arc length proportion?
 
A.  (central angle / 360) = arc/circumference = sector/total area. If the total is given, take the fraction — no
 need to find r.
Q.  Which shape has the largest area for a given perimeter?
 
A.  A circle (largest area, least perimeter). Among polygons, the regular one maximizes area.
 
Q.  To maximize a polygon’s area for fixed sides, what shape?
 
A.  Make it regular (all sides/angles equal). For a quadrilateral, max area is a square.
 
Q.  In a regular hexagon, how does the radius relate to the side?
 
A.  A regular hexagon = 6 equilateral triangles; the radius of its circumscribed circle equals its side length.
 
Areas & solids
Q.  Area of parallelogram / rhombus / rectangle?
 
A.  base × height.
 
Q.  Area of a trapezoid?
 
A.  [(b1 + b2)/2] × h (average of parallel sides × height).
 
Q.  Cube: surface area, volume, longest diagonal?
 
A.  SA = 6a2; Vol = s3; diagonal = x√3.
 
Q.  Cuboid: surface area, volume, longest diagonal?
 
A.  SA = 2(ab+bc+ca); Vol = abc; diagonal = √(x2+y2+z2).
 
Q.  Cylinder: volume and surface area?
 
A.  Vol = πr2h; SA = 2πr(r+h).
 
7   STATISTICS
Q.  Mean, median, mode?
 
A.  Mean = average; median = middle value; mode = most frequent value.
 
Q.  Mean of an evenly-spaced set?
 
A.  (first term + last term) / 2 (= median too).
 
Q.  Range?
 
A.  Greatest − least.
 
Q.  What does standard deviation measure?
 
A.  Spread — the typical distance of data points from the mean. Higher = more scattered.
 
Q.  Can SD be negative? When is it 0?
 
A.  Never negative (≥ 0). It’s 0 only when all values are identical.
 
Q.  Effect on SD of adding/subtracting a constant?
 
A.  Unchanged (shifts the data, doesn’t change spread).
 
Q.  Effect on SD of multiplying/dividing by k?
 
A.  SD scales by the same factor k.
 
Q.  The empirical rule (68-95-99.7)?
 
A.  ±1 SD → 68%, ±2 SD → 95%, ±3 SD → 99.7% of data.
 
Q.  How does 68% split around the mean?
 
A.  34% on each side (the curve is symmetric).

Q.  Find the value range for a given % (mean µ, SD σ)?
 
A.  mean ± (n × SD), where n = 1 (68%), 2 (95%), 3 (99.7%).
 
Q.  Z-score formula and meaning?
 
A.  z = (x − µ)/σ = how many SDs a value is from the mean (− below, + above).
 
Q.  Percentiles for z = 0, +1, −2?
 
A.  z=0 → 50th; z=+1 → 84th; z=−2 → ~2.5th.
 
Q.  In a normal curve, what does the mean control?
 
A.  Location — where the peak sits (bigger → right). 50% below, 50% above.
 
Q.  In a normal curve, what does SD control?
 
A.  Width/spread: small σ → tall & narrow; large σ → short & wide. Area always = 1.
 
Q.  Discrete uniform distribution — probability of one outcome?
 
A.  1/n (n equally likely outcomes, e.g. a die → 1/6).
 
Q.  Continuous uniform (a to b): height, mean?
 
A.  Height = 1/(b−a); mean = median = (a+b)/2. Probability = area; P(sub-range) = length × height.
 
Quartiles, percentiles & spread
Q.  How do quartiles divide a dataset?
 
A.  Into 4 equal groups. Q1 = lower 25% boundary, Q2 = median (50%), Q3 = upper 75% boundary.
 
Q.  How do you find Q1, Q2, Q3?
 
A.  Q2 = median of the whole set. Q1 = median of the lower half. Q3 = median of the upper half.
 
Q.  Interquartile range (IQR)?
 
A.  Q3 − Q1 (the spread of the middle 50% of the data).
 
Q.  What does a boxplot show?
 
A.  The five-number summary: lowest, Q1, Q2 (median), Q3, highest — the box spans Q1 to Q3.
 
Q.  Percentile rank formula?
 
A.  (number of values below x / total number of values) × 100 = % of data points strictly below that value.
 
Q.  What is standardization?
 
A.  Re-expressing a value as how many SDs it sits from the mean: subtract the mean, divide by the SD (= the
 z-score).
8   COUNTING & PROBABILITY
Q.  Permutation vs combination — the key difference?
 
A.  Permutation: order matters (ABC ≠ BCA). Combination: order doesn’t (ABC = BCA).
 
Q.  Permutation formula (n items, choose r)?
 
A.  nPr = n! / (n−r)!.
 
Q.  Combination formula (n items, choose r)?
 
A.  nCr = n! / [r!(n−r)!] (= permutation ÷ r!).
 
Q.  Permutations with repeated items?
 
A.  n! / (r1! · r2! …), dividing by the factorial of each repeated group.
 
Q.  Circular arrangements of n items?
 
A.  (n−1)!
 
Q.  Handshake formula (n people, each shakes once)?
 
A.  n(n−1)/2 (= nC2).
 
Q.  Counting paths / arrangements with m of one type, n of another?
 
A.  (m+n)! / (m! · n!).
 
Q.  Overlapping sets — two sets?
 
A.  Total = A + B − (both) + (neither).
 
Q.  Overlapping sets — three sets?
 
A.  Total = A + B + C − (3 pairwise overlaps) + (all three) + (neither).

Q.  ‘Exactly one’ vs ‘at least one’ in sets?
 
A.  ‘At least one’ = in any set (the union). ‘Exactly one’ = in one set only, excluding the overlaps. Draw a Venn
 diagram and subtract the overlap regions.
Q.  Basic probability?
 
A.  Favorable outcomes / total outcomes.
 
Q.  Complement rule?
 
A.  P(not A) = 1 − P(A).
 
Q.  P(A or B) — general formula?
 
A.  P(A) + P(B) − P(A and B).
 
Q.  Mutually exclusive events — meaning & P(A and B)?
 
A.  Can’t happen at the same time; P(A and B) = 0; so P(A or B) = P(A)+P(B).
 
Q.  Independent events — meaning & P(A and B)?
 
A.  One doesn’t affect the other; P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B).
 
Q.  Why do we multiply for AND (independent)?
 
A.  ‘Both’ narrows possibilities — a fraction of a fraction. Each condition contributes one factor.
 
Q.  Expected value formula?
 
A.  E[X] = Σ(outcome × probability) = long-run average.
 
Q.  Expected value of a game — interpretation?
 
A.  (net gain × P(win)) + (net loss × P(lose)). >0 worth playing; <0 don’t; =0 fair.
 
Q.  Binomial conditions (BINS)?
 
A.  Binary outcomes, Independent trials, Number of trials fixed, Same success probability.
 
Q.  Binomial probability formula?
 
A.  P(k in n) = nCk · pk · (1−p)n−k = (ways to arrange) × (success)k × (failure)n−k.

Ratios & proportions
Q.  What is a ratio, and how do you scale it?
 
A.  A comparison a:b. Multiply/divide both parts by the same number to scale (2:3 = 4:6 = 6:9). Reduce to
 lowest terms to compare.
Q.  How do you combine ratios (a:b and b:c)?
 
A.  Make the shared term match, then chain. If A:B = 2:3 and B:C = 4:5, scale B to a common value (12):
 A:B:C = 8:12:15.
Q.  Direct vs inverse proportion?
 
A.  Direct: y = kx (both grow together; y/x constant). Inverse: y = k/x (one grows as the other shrinks; xy
 constant).
Q.  Splitting a quantity in a ratio?
 
A.  Add the ratio parts to get total parts, then give each its share. Split 60 in 2:3 → 5 parts → 24 and 36.
 
Percents (advanced)
Q.  Successive percent changes — can you just add them?
 
A.  No. Apply multipliers in sequence. +20% then −20% = ×1.2 ×0.8 = ×0.96 = a 4% net DECREASE (not 0%).
 
Q.  Percent of a percent?
 
A.  Multiply the decimals. 30% of 40% = 0.30 × 0.40 = 0.12 = 12%.
 
Q.  ‘A is what percent of B’ vs ‘A is what percent greater than B’?
 
A.  ‘Percent of’ = A/B ×100. ‘Percent greater than’ = (A−B)/B ×100 (the change relative to B).

Rates: speed, distance, work
Q.  Distance / speed / time relationship?
 
A.  Distance = speed × time. (Rearrange: speed = d/t, time = d/s.)
 
Q.  Average speed for a round trip?
 
A.  Total distance / total time — NOT the average of the speeds. For equal distances at speeds a, b:
 2ab/(a+b).
Q.  Combined work rate (two workers together)?
 
A.  Add their rates. If A does a job in x hrs and B in y hrs, together: rate = 1/x + 1/y, so time = 1/(1/x + 1/y) =
 xy/(x+y).
Q.  Relative speed — same vs opposite directions?
 
A.  Opposite directions → ADD speeds (closing faster). Same direction → SUBTRACT speeds.
 
Absolute value & number line
Q.  What does |x| mean?
 
A.  Distance from 0 on the number line; always ≥ 0. |−5| = 5, |5| = 5.
 
Q.  How to solve |x − 3| = 5?
 
A.  Split into two cases: x − 3 = 5 (x = 8) OR x − 3 = −5 (x = −2).
 
Q.  How to solve |x| < 5 (an inequality)?
 
A.  It means −5 < x < 5 (within 5 of zero). |x| > 5 means x < −5 OR x > 5 (outside).
 
Q.  |a + b| vs |a| + |b|?
 
A.  |a + b| ≤ |a| + |b| (triangle inequality). |ab| = |a|·|b| (always equal).
 
Probability shortcuts
Q.  ‘At least one’ probability — the fast way?
 
A.  P(at least one) = 1 − P(none). Far easier than adding up all the ‘one or more’ cases.
 
Q.  Probability with/without replacement?
 
A.  Without replacement, the total shrinks each draw (events become dependent). With replacement,
 probabilities stay constant (independent).
Data interpretation (a whole GRE question type)
Q.  What is a Data Interpretation set?
 
A.  A group of questions based on one or more charts/tables (bar, line, pie, scatter). You read values off the
 graphic and compute.
Q.  Reading a pie chart — key relationship?
 
A.  Each slice’s % × the total = that category’s value. The whole circle = 100% = 360°.
 
Q.  Common DI traps?
 
A.  Watch axis scales (do they start at 0?), units (thousands vs millions), and whether a question asks for a
 value vs a percent change. Re-read what’s actually being asked.
GRE-specific strategy
Q.  What is a Quantitative Comparison (QC) question?
 
A.  You compare Quantity A vs Quantity B. Four fixed answers: A bigger / B bigger / equal / cannot be
 determined. Roughly half the quant section.
Q.  QC strategy — the key habit?
 
A.  Try to make the two quantities equal, then test if other values break it. Plug in 0, 1, a fraction, and a
 negative — if the relationship changes, the answer is ‘cannot be determined.’
Q.  QC — the ‘cannot be determined’ trap?
 
A.  If a variable can be negative, a fraction, or zero, the relationship often flips. Never assume variables are
 positive integers unless stated.
Q.  Numeric Entry questions?
 
A.  You type the answer (no options to eliminate). Watch the requested form: fraction vs decimal, rounding
 instructions, units.

Q.  Multiple-answer questions?
 
A.  ‘Select all that apply’ — there may be more than one correct choice, and you must get all of them. Check
 every option independently.
Q.  Smart use of the on-screen calculator?
 
A.  It’s basic (no order-of-operations memory beyond simple chains). Estimate first; use it for arithmetic, not as
 a crutch. QC often needs reasoning, not computation.
Q.  Plugging in numbers — when and how?
 
A.  For variable-heavy or QC problems, substitute simple test numbers (0, 1, 2, −1, ½). Use them to check
 relationships and eliminate answers.
Q.  Back-solving from answer choices?
 
A.  For ‘solve for x’ multiple-choice, plug the answer options into the problem and see which works — often
 faster than algebra.
A few more commonly-tested facts
Q.  Sum of the first n positive integers?
 
A.  n(n+1)/2 (e.g. 1+2+…+100 = 5050).
 
Q.  Sum of first n even / odd numbers?
 
A.  First n even = n(n+1). First n odd = n2.
 
Q.  Prime numbers under 20?
 
A.  2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19. (2 is the only even prime; 1 is NOT prime.)
 
Q.  Quick squares to know (11–15)?
 
A.  112=121, 122=144, 132=169, 142=196, 152=225.
 
Q.  Fraction–decimal–percent conversions worth memorizing?
 
A.  1/8=12.5%, 1/4=25%, 1/3≈33.3%, 3/8=37.5%, 1/2=50%, 2/3≈66.7%, 5/8=62.5%, 3/4=75%.
 
Q.  Is a negative number raised to an even vs odd power positive or negative?
 
A.  Even power → positive ((−2)2=4). Odd power → negative ((−2)3=−8).
